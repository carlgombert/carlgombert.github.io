Networking Study Reference

1. Introduction to the Internet

The Internet is a global "network of networks" that provides an infrastructure for distributed applications and services. It connects billions of computing devices, including:

  • Traditional Hosts: PCs, laptops, smartphones, servers.
  • IoT / Embedded Devices: IP picture frames, Slingbox (remote cable control), Web-enabled toasters, AR devices, sensorized mattresses, connected cars, scooters, and gaming devices.
  • Advanced Applications: 5G/NextG Teleoperation, where command-and-control (C&C) data uses the downlink (DL) and vehicle sensor data (camera feeds) uses the uplink (UL).

1.1 Perspectives on the Internet

  • Hardware/Software View: A collection of interconnected devices (hosts, routers, links) and protocols (HTTP, TCP, IP, etc.) that control the sending and receiving of messages.
  • Service View: An infrastructure that provides services to applications (Web, streaming, email, social media) and provides a programming interface ("hooks") for distributed applications to connect.

1.2 Key Components

  • Protocols: Protocols are everywhere. They define the format, order of messages sent and received among network entities, and actions taken on message transmission or receipt. Examples include HTTP, TCP, IP, WiFi, 4G/5G, and Ethernet.
  • Internet Standards: Maintained by the IETF (Internet Engineering Task Force) through RFCs (Request for Comments).

2. Network Structure

The network is divided into three main components:

  1. Network Edge: Hosts (clients and servers), often residing in data centers.
  2. Access Networks: The physical media (wired or wireless communication links) that connect the edge devices to the first router (edge router).
  3. Network Core: A mesh of interconnected routers; the "network of networks" that ties everything together (e.g., national or global ISPs, content provider networks).

3. Naming and Addressing

Networking is more than connecting nodes; it requires a way to identify participants in a shared resource.

  • Address: A byte-string that identifies a specific node.
  • Addressing Types:
    • Unicast: Delivery to a single, specific node.
    • Broadcast: Delivery to all nodes in the network.
    • Multicast: Delivery to a specific subset of nodes in the network.

4. Network Core: Switching and Multiplexing

The network core is responsible for moving data through the mesh of routers. This is achieved through two primary methods: Circuit Switching and Packet Switching.

4.1 Circuit Switching

In circuit switching, a dedicated end-to-end connection ("circuit") is established before any data is sent.

  • Characteristics:
    • Reserved Resources: Bandwidth and switch capacity are dedicated to the "call."
    • Guaranteed Performance: No interference from other users (no jitter/congestion once connected).
    • Idle Resources: If the user isn't sending data, the allocated circuit remains idle and cannot be used by others.
    • Call Blocking: Occurs if all available circuits are already in use.
  • Analogy: The original telephone network; railroads and trains (fixed tracks).

[!IMPORTANT] Is Circuit Switching ever better? Yes. While packet switching is generally more efficient for "bursty" traffic (like web browsing), circuit switching is superior for applications requiring guaranteed, constant bandwidth and zero queuing delay, such as high-quality real-time video or dedicated communication lines.

Multiplexing in Circuit Switching

Resources are divided into "pieces" using:

  • Frequency Division Multiplexing (FDM): The total electromagnetic or optical frequency spectrum is divided into narrow bands. Each call is allocated its own band.
  • Time Division Multiplexing (TDM): Time is divided into slots. Each call is allocated a periodic time slot during which it can transmit at the maximum rate of the wider frequency band.

4.2 Packet Switching

In packet switching, hosts break application-layer messages into small chunks called packets. Packets from different users share network resources on demand.

  • Characteristics:
    • Store-and-Forward: The entire packet (L bits) must arrive at a router before it can be transmitted on the next link (rate R bps). Transmission delay = L/R.
    • Statistical Multiplexing: Resources are used as needed; packets from different sources are interleaved on the link.
  • Best-Effort Service: The network core provides a connectionless "datagram" service that does not guarantee delivery, order, or timing. Reliability is handled by the edges.
  • Queuing and Loss: If the arrival rate (bps) to a link exceeds its transmission rate, packets will queue in the router's buffer (memory). If the buffer fills up, packets are dropped (lost).
  • Analogy: The Internet; highways and cars (shared, dynamic routing).

4.3 Packet Switching Advantage: Statistical Multiplexing

Packet switching is often more efficient than circuit switching because it allows more users to share the same resources.

Example Scenario:

  • Link: 1 Mbps.
  • Each User: Requires 100 kbps when active, but is only active 10% of the time.
  • Circuit Switching: Can only support 10 users (100 kbps each, statically allocated).
  • Packet Switching: Can support significantly more users with a very low probability of congestion. If there are 35 users, the probability that more than 10 are active at the same time is extremely small (approx. 0.0004).

Conclusion: Packet switching allows for more efficient use of network resources by exploiting the "bursty" nature of data traffic.

Probability of Congestion

To calculate the probability that exactly $k$ out of $n$ users are active (where each user is active with probability $p$): $$P(k) = \binom{n}{k} p^k (1-p)^{n-k}$$ The probability of congestion is the sum of probabilities where $k$ exceeds the network's capacity.

4.4 Link Utilization

Link Utilization is the percentage of a link's total capacity that is currently being used.

  • Formula: $U = (\text{Total Arrival Rate}) / (\text{Link Capacity}) \times 100%$
  • Example: If two clients each send at 10 Mbps into a router that has a 100 Mbps output link, the utilization is $(10 + 10) / 100 = 20%$.

4.5 Routing vs. Forwarding

These are the two key network-layer functions performed in the core:

  • Forwarding (Switching): A local action. It involves moving arriving packets from a router's input link to the appropriate output link based on a local forwarding table.
  • Routing: A global action. It involves determining the end-to-end paths (source-to-destination) taken by packets using routing algorithms.

5. Network Performance Metrics

Network performance is primarily measured by how fast and reliably data moves from point A to point B.

5.1 Throughput

Throughput is the rate (bits/time unit) at which bits are being sent from sender to receiver.

  • Instantaneous Throughput: The rate at a specific point in time.
  • Average Throughput: The rate over a longer period of time.
  • Bottleneck Link: The link in an end-to-end path that constrains the total throughput.
    • If a server sends at rate $R_s$ and a client receives at rate $R_c$, the end-to-end throughput is $\min(R_s, R_c)$.
    • In a shared backbone with capacity $R$ shared by $N$ connections, the throughput is $\min(R_s, R_c, R/N)$.

5.2 Latency (Delay)

The total Nodal Delay at a single router is the sum of four components: $$d_{nodal} = d_{proc} + d_{queue} + d_{trans} + d_{prop}$$

  1. Processing Delay ($d_{proc}$): Time spent checking for bit errors and determining the output link. Usually microseconds.
  2. Queuing Delay ($d_{queue}$): Time spent waiting at the output link for transmission. Depends on the congestion level of the router.
  3. Transmission Delay ($d_{trans}$): The time required to push all the packet's bits into the link.
    • Formula: $d_{trans} = L / R$ (where $L$ is packet length in bits and $R$ is link bandwidth in bps).
  4. Propagation Delay ($d_{prop}$): The time it takes for a bit to travel from the beginning of the link to the next router.
    • Formula: $d_{prop} = d / s$ (where $d$ is the length of the physical link and $s$ is the propagation speed in the medium, approx. $2 \times 10^8$ m/sec).

[!TIP] The Caravan Analogy Think of a 10-car caravan as a packet and each car as a bit.

  • Toll Booth: The communication link.
  • Toll Service Time: Transmission delay (time to "push" the cars onto the road).
  • Highway Travel: Propagation delay (time for cars to travel between booths).
  • If the cars travel very fast (high propagation speed) but the toll booth is slow (low transmission rate), cars may arrive at the next booth before the whole caravan has even left the first one.

5.3 Measuring Delay: Traceroute

Traceroute is a diagnostic tool that provides delay measurements from a source to every router along the end-to-end path.

  • It sends three packets to each router $i$ along the path.
  • Each router returns the packets to the sender.
  • The sender measures the time interval between transmission and reply to determine the RTT (Round Trip Time).

6. Protocol Layering and the TCP/IP Stack

The Internet handles its immense complexity through a layered architecture. Each layer provides a service to the layer above it by using the services of the layer below.

6.1 The 5-Layer Internet Stack

  1. Application Layer: Supports network applications (e.g., HTTP, SMTP, FTP, DNS). The data unit is a Message.
  2. Transport Layer: Handles process-to-process data transfer (e.g., TCP for reliability, UDP). The data unit is a Segment.
  3. Network Layer: Routes datagrams from source host to destination host (e.g., IP, routing protocols). The data unit is a Datagram.
  4. Link Layer: Handles data transfer between neighboring network elements (e.g., Ethernet, 802.11 WiFi, PPP). The data unit is a Frame.
  5. Physical Layer: Carries the actual bits "on the wire."

6.2 Key Concepts in Layering

  • Protocols vs. Services:
    • Service: What a layer does (the functionality it provides to the layer above).
    • Protocol: The rules/messages exchanged between peering entities (e.g., two Transport layers on different hosts) to implement a service. Protocols define:
    • Format of messages.
    • Order of messages (e.g., a "Hi-Hi" Handshake before data exchange).
    • Actions taken on message transmission or receipt.
  • Interfaces:
    • Service Interface: Operations to access the layer's service (used by the layer above).
    • Peer-to-Peer Interface: The protocol used for communication between peers.
  • PDUs vs. SDUs:
    • SDU (Service Data Unit): The data packet handed to a layer by the layer above.
    • PDU (Protocol Data Unit): The packet exchanged between peers. It consists of the SDU plus a layer-specific header (e.g., $PDU = SDU + Header$).

6.3 Encapsulation (The Matryoshka Doll Model)

As data moves down the stack at the sender:

  1. Application creates a Message.
  2. Transport encapsulates it with a header ($H_t$) to create a Segment.
  3. Network encapsulates the segment with a header ($H_n$) to create a Datagram.
  4. Link encapsulates the datagram with a header ($H_l$) and potentially a trailer to create a Frame. At the receiver, the process is reversed (Decapsulation).

7. Internet Architectural Principles

7.1 The Hourglass Model

The Internet architecture is often described as an hourglass.

  • The Narrow Waist: IP (Internet Protocol) sits in the middle. Everything runs over IP, and IP runs over everything.
  • This allows for diverse applications at the top and diverse physical technologies at the bottom to coexist.

7.2 "Smart Edges, Dumb Core"

A fundamental design principle of the Internet:

  • Dumb Core: The network core (routers) remains simple. Its primary job is "best-effort" packet switching (addressing, routing, and forwarding).
  • Smart Edges: Complex functionalities like error recovery, flow control, and congestion control are implemented at the end systems (hosts).

8. Network Challenges and Solutions

8.1 Common Errors

  • Bit-level errors: Caused by electrical interference.
  • Frame-level errors: Collisions or contention at the link layer.
  • Packet-level errors: Delay or loss due to congestion/buffer overflow.
  • Out-of-order delivery: Packets taking different paths through the network.
  • Link/Node failures: System crashes or physical cable cuts.

8.2 Mitigation Mechanisms

To "fill the gap" between application expectations and underlying technology, the following are used:

  • Redundancy: Adding bits to detect and correct errors.
  • Acknowledgements (ACKs) & Retransmissions: Recovering lost data.
  • Sequence Numbers: Ensuring packets are reordered correctly at the receiver.
  • Adaptive Routing: Sensing failures and routing around them.

10. Message Segmentation and Pipelining

In modern networks, long application-layer messages (e.g., a 10 MB image) are broken into smaller packets (segmentation) before being sent.

10.1 The Pipelining Effect

Segmentation allows for pipelining: while the first switch is forwarding packet 1 to the second switch, the source host can already be sending packet 2 to the first switch. This significantly reduces total end-to-end delay compared to sending the entire message as one giant block.

10.2 Delay Formulas

For a message of size $M$ bits, segmented into $P$ packets of length $L$ bits each, sent over $N$ links of rate $R$ bps:

  1. Without Segmentation: $$T_{total} = N \times (M / R)$$ (The entire message must be stored-and-forwarded at every hop.)

  2. With Segmentation (General Formula): $$T_{total} = (N + P - 1) \times (L / R)$$

    • Derivation: It takes $L/R$ to push the first packet to the first switch. It takes $(N-1) \times (L/R)$ for the first packet to reach the destination. Meanwhile, the remaining $(P-1)$ packets follow immediately behind, each arriving $L/R$ after the previous one.
    • Note: This ignores propagation and processing delays. If included, add $N \times d_{prop}$.

10.3 Pros and Cons of Segmentation

Advantages Disadvantages
Reduced Delay: Pipelining speeds up delivery. Header Overhead: Every packet needs its own header (metadata).
Error Management: If a packet is corrupted, only that small packet needs to be retransmitted, not the whole message. Reassembly Complexity: The receiver must put the packets back in the correct order.
Fairness: Prevents one large file from hogging a link for a long duration. Processing Load: Routers must process many small packets instead of one large one.
